DATA 201#

Describing Univariate Data with Statistics and Plots#

Before building a model or doing any analysis, it‚Äôs important to understand the data you are working with. This includes things such as understanding what was measured, how the data was collected, being able to produce useful summaries of the data, and determine if there are any potential problems/mistakes in the data.

Additionally, these things are also useful when you are trying to interpret the results of someone else‚Äôs analysis.

You should ALWAYS, ALWAYS look at your data. Don‚Äôt assume that any method you are trying to use automatically works for the type of data you have.

Summary statistics#

The most commonly used summary statistics tend to come in two broad categories:

  1. Measures of central tendency: what is a ‚Äútypical‚Äù observation? (mean, median, mode)

  2. Measures of variability: how spread out is the data around the center? (stdev, IQR)

The interquartile range (IQR) is a measure of statistical dispersion, or the spread of the data, in descriptive statistics. Aka the middle 50%.

Measures of variation (spread)#

Next, we‚Äôll look at some measures of variation. While the goal of means and medians is to give a sense of the ‚Äúmiddle‚Äù of the data, measures of variation tell us how far away from the middle the data tends to be.

The most common measure is the variance, which is the average of the squared differences between each data point and the mean. Mathematically, if \(X=\{x_1,x_2,...,x_N\}\) is a set of \(N\) observations, then:

\[Var[X] = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i-\mu)^2\]

where \(\mu\) is the mean. You will also often see the symbol \(\sigma^2\) used to denote the variance.

# (referred to the above) why is that?

It is common for people to look at the standard deviation, which is just the square root of the variance (and is often denoted as \(\sigma\)). The benefit of this is that the standard deviation and the mean have the same physical units, so they are more directly comparable. Note that the variance and standard deviation ‚Äúpair with‚Äù the mean - they are the appropriate measures of variation to report when you report the mean.

np.std(college_2016['num_students'])

22554.990072279823

Taking the ratio of standard deviation / mean is called the coefficient of variation.

np.std(college_2016['num_students'])/np.mean(college_2016['num_students'])

0.9335185985904977

Quartiles and Box Plots#

Quartiles are computed by sorting the data and splitting it into 4 equally sized groups (hence ‚Äúquart-‚Äù, meaning 4). The numbers reported are the values which split each of these groups (see this wikipedia page for some example computations). In other words, the quartiles of a data set are the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles, since those three values divide the data into 4 equally sized groups.

Let‚Äôs compute the quartiles of the number of students in 2016:
